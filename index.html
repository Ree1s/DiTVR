DiTVR: Zero-Shot Diffusion Transformer for Video Restoration

Anonymous Authors
Paper ID 110

Our method is capable of performing multiple video restoration tasks including super-resolution, denoising, deblurring, and colorization.

Abstract

Video restoration aims to reconstruct high-quality video sequences from low-quality inputs, addressing tasks such as super-resolution, denoising, and deblurring. Traditional regression-based methods often produce unrealistic details and require extensive paired datasets, while generative diffusion models face challenges in ensuring temporal consistency. To overcome these limitations, we introduce an anonymous zero-shot video restoration framework that leverages a pre-trained Diffusion Transformer operating directly in pixel space. Unlike prior methods that utilize optical flow solely for structural guidance, our framework integrates a spatiotemporal neighbor cache, trajectory-aware attention, and a flow-guided diffusion sampler, all driven by optical flow trajectories extracted from low-quality inputs. These components enhance spatial alignment and robust temporal coherence across frames. Additionally, our flow-guided diffusion sampler refines neglected detail improvement during inference, ensuring temporal alignment and realistic detail simultaneously. Extensive experiments show that the proposed method achieves state-of-the-art performance on zero-shot video restoration benchmarks, excelling in temporal consistency and high-fidelity restoration without task-specific training.

Memory Usage Comparison

Method Memory
Baseline OOM
Our STNC + Flash Attention 9.54GB
Extended Attention 11.01GB
Our TAttn with STNC 3.68GB

Our STNC + Flash Attention reduces self-Attention memory from OOM to 9.54GB. For temporal Attention, zero-shot methods like TokenFlow require 11.01GB with extended Attention, while our TAttn with STNC uses only 3.68GB. By selecting matching patches via flow trajectories, our temporal cache supports both stages, enabling block-based flash Attention and ensuring temporal consistency with minimal memory overhead.

Optical Flow Model Comparison on Vid4 Dataset

Method Warping Error Difference
Ours (GMFlow vs RAFT) 1.08 (lower)
DiffIR2VR (GMFlow vs RAFT) 1.648 (lower)

Our method shows better robustness across different optical flow models. When switching from RAFT to GMFlow, our approach achieves a warping error reduction of 1.08, while DiffIR2VR shows a larger reduction of 1.648. This demonstrates that our method is more robust and consistent across different flow estimation techniques, as it shows smaller performance variations when changing the underlying optical flow model.
